The agent you choose has more impact on your sale or purchase than almost any other decision — they shape your price, your timeline, and how stressful the whole thing feels. Yet most people pick one the hardest way possible: a name they half-remember, a sign in a yard, or whoever a search engine surfaced first. Here's how to choose well.

## Start with local experience

The most important quality isn't the size of the brokerage or how many billboards you've seen — it's whether the agent actively works *your* area. Southeast Wisconsin is dozens of micro-markets. Pricing, buyer expectations, and which improvements pay off differ between Wauwatosa, Cedarburg, Kenosha, and the lakefront. An agent who closes deals in your neighborhood every month knows things a distant agent simply can't.

Ask directly: *How many homes have you sold in this area in the last year?*

## Questions worth asking

Before you commit, have a real conversation. Strong answers to these tell you a lot:

- **How will you price my home, and what are the recent comparable sales?** You want data-driven reasoning, not a flattering number designed to win your listing.
- **What's your marketing plan?** Professional photography and MLS exposure are the baseline; ask what else they do.
- **How will you communicate, and how often?** Mismatched communication styles are a top source of frustration.
- **What's your experience with situations like mine?** First-time seller, relocation, downsizing, a home that needs work — experience with your specific scenario matters.
- **Can you share recent client references?** A confident agent will.

## Red flags to watch for

- **The highest suggested price wins your business.** Some agents inflate the price to get the listing, then push for cuts later. Be wary of a number far above the comparable sales.
- **Vague or evasive answers** about their track record in your area.
- **Poor communication during courtship.** If they're slow to respond now, it won't improve once they have your signature.
- **Pressure to sign immediately.** A good agent earns the relationship; they don't rush it.

## Big brand vs. local pro

National brand recognition can feel reassuring, but the brand doesn't sell your home — the individual agent does. A well-connected local agent with deep neighborhood knowledge often serves you better than a famous name who works your area only occasionally. Judge the person, not the logo.

## Don't skip the rapport

You'll work closely with this person through a stressful, high-stakes process. Beyond competence, you want someone who listens, explains clearly, and whose judgment you trust. Technical skill matters, but so does feeling genuinely represented.
